Case Study 01

Burger King: Reclaim the Flame

What happened?

Burger King found themselves falling behind other fast food giants and needed a major change. Instead of making a typical, glossy ad that pretended everything was perfect, they chose to be completely honest. Their "Reclaim the Flame" campaign openly admitted that their stores were outdated and their menus were too confusing. They put real money behind this reset, remodeling locations, upgrading their digital app, and putting the focus right back on their legendary Whopper.

Why it worked

They brought this strategy to life during massive events like the Oscars, sharing a transparent story about their journey back to the top. To make it stick, they paired this honesty with an incredibly simple, catchy jingle, the famous "Whopper, Whopper" song, that quickly took over social media. The big takeaway here is that audiences respect honesty. When a brand admits its flaws and shows a real plan to fix them, people naturally build a stronger bond with it.

Visual appearance

The visuals rely heavily on retro-styled, thick typography with a warm color palette of deep browns, vintage oranges, and rich reds. Advertisements feature close-up shots of a real, unglamorous flame-broiled Whopper, highlighting fresh ingredients rather than hyper-stylized plastic props. The store remodels showcase clean lines, natural wood elements, and a minimalist variation of their classic logo.

Case Study 02

McDonald's: Viral CEO Moment

What happened?

Big companies usually spend weeks editing and scripting any video that features their top executives. McDonald's did the exact opposite when a slightly awkward, unpolished video of their CEO went viral. Instead of panicking and scrubbing the clip from the internet, the brand completely leaned into the moment and joined the conversation online.

Why it worked

By refusing to take themselves too seriously, the corporate leadership suddenly felt human and relatable. They let the internet make memes and play with the clip, which worked perfectly because modern social media algorithms heavily reward raw, authentic content over corporate polish. This proves that pulling back the curtain and showing real, unedited moments can earn far more attention and organic reach than an expensive, over-produced commercial.

Visual appearance

The video looks like a casual, handheld smartphone recording rather than a cinematic production. The lighting is standard office fluorescent, and the framing is a simple medium shot of the CEO speaking directly to the camera with natural, slightly unpolished body language. It looks identical to organic user-generated content found on standard social media feeds.

Case Study 03

Connecticut Tourism: Pizza Capital of the US

What happened?

New York and Chicago are traditionally known as the ultimate pizza capitals of America. Connecticut's tourism board decided to shake things up by boldly claiming that Connecticut, specifically New Haven, is the true home of the best pizza in the country. They placed giant billboards right near the New York border and made official government declarations to stir the pot, causing massive online arguments and outrage from proud New Yorkers.

Why it worked

This worked because they picked a fun, low-stakes controversy that people naturally love to debate. The online bickering caused major news outlets and famous food influencers to travel to the state just to test the pizza themselves. This brought in millions of dollars of free publicity. Taking a daring, opinionated stance can be a powerful way to break through the noise and get everyone talking about your brand.

Visual appearance

The campaign centers around giant, minimal highway billboards with bright, contrasting backgrounds and bold white text that reads "Pizza Capital of the United States." Images often display authentic, char-crusted, brick-oven New Haven-style pizzas served on simple white wax paper or inside traditional brown cardboard boxes.

Case Study 04

Svedka: AI-Generated Super Bowl Ad

What happened?

Svedka Vodka decided to tap into the massive global conversation surrounding Artificial Intelligence during the Super Bowl. They brought back their classic, futuristic "fembot" character, but they used advanced AI tools to generate the imagery. Because AI art can look slightly unusual and dreamlike, it immediately caught people's eyes during a broadcast filled with traditional commercials.

Why it worked

Instead of playing it safe, Svedka used a trending technology to position themselves as a forward-thinking, innovative brand. The slightly strange look of the ad sparked instant debates online about the use of AI in advertising, which only amplified their visibility. If you can find a creative way to tie a cutting-edge trend directly to your brand's existing identity, the sheer curiosity of the public will pull eyes toward your product.

Visual appearance

The commercial is filled with surreal, ultra-glossy, metallic textures and vibrant neon blue and purple lighting. The central character is a sleek, silver cybernetic figure with stylized features, moving through a futuristic, hyper-realistic lounge setting that looks distinctly computer-generated and dreamlike.

Case Study 05

Burberry: Portraits of an Icon

What happened?

The luxury fashion house Burberry wanted to remind the world of their deep roots and timeless quality. Rather than pushing a collection of trendy new designs, they built an entire campaign around their most legendary item, the classic British trench coat. They brought together a diverse group of British icons, ranging from legendary older actors to trending musicians, and photographed them all wearing the exact same style of coat.

Why it worked

This strategy succeeded because it focused on heritage marketing. It reminded buyers that purchasing a Burberry coat is an investment in history and craftsmanship rather than just a fleeting fashion trend. The premium, artistic execution of the photos reinforced their luxury status. It shows that your brand's history can be your greatest competitive advantage if you tell its story correctly.

Visual appearance

The imagery consists of high-contrast, black-and-white portrait photography alongside muted, earthy-toned color shots. The backgrounds are simple, clean studio backdrops or historic British streets, keeping the focus entirely on the crisp lines, fabric textures, and distinct plaid linings of the classic beige trench coats.

Case Study 06

Chips Ahoy x Stranger Things

What happened?

Chips Ahoy wanted to capture the attention of younger generations while pulling in older audiences who love nostalgia. To do this, they partnered with Netflix's massive hit series, Stranger Things. Since the show is famous for its 1980s retro aesthetic and incredibly loyal fan base, Chips Ahoy completely redesigned their packaging and digital ads to look like they came straight out of the show's mysterious universe.

Why it worked

This collaboration allowed Chips Ahoy to borrow an enormous, highly active audience instead of trying to build excitement from scratch. They didn't just slap a logo on their box; they integrated the product into the fan experience. Tapping into popular culture and aligning your product with communities that are already passionate is one of the fastest ways to boost your brand's relevance.

Visual appearance

The product packaging features a dark, moody blue and deep red color scheme inspired by the show's "Upside Down" dimension. The classic blue Chips Ahoy logo is stylized with cracks and eerie neon glows, accompanied by illustrations of the show's characters and signature monsters emerging around the cookies.

Case Study 07

Duolingo: Death of Duo Owl

What happened?

Duolingo has built a massive social media following by making their green owl mascot, Duo, act funny, dramatic, and slightly unhinged on TikTok. They took this storytelling to an extreme level by running a multi-day stunt where they claimed the mascot had been killed off. Fans reacted with shock, grief, and comedy, keeping a close eye on the app until the brand eventually revived him in another theatrical twist.

Why it worked

This worked because Duolingo treats its brand like an entertaining TV show rather than a software product. They understood that their audience loves internet meme culture and dark humor, so they leaned into that tone completely. When you transform your brand into an entertaining character with a distinct personality, customers will follow your journey out of genuine enjoyment rather than just buying a service.

Visual appearance

The social media posts use a simple, vibrant cartoon art style featuring the bright green Duo owl. During the stunt, the visuals shifted to humorous, overly dramatic funeral setups, complete with candlelight, black mourning veils on the cartoon character, and mock memorial graphics shared across TikTok and Instagram.

Case Study 08

Airbnb: Icons

What happened?

Airbnb recognized that travelers were starting to feel fatigued by standard vacation rentals. To bring the magic back to their platform, they launched a special category called "Icons." They built real, fully functional, and highly detailed physical replicas of famous homes from pop culture, including the floating house from the movie Up, Shrek's swamp, and the X-Men mansion, allowing lucky users to actually book stays in them.

Why it worked

This campaign completely changed how people view Airbnb, shifting its image from a simple utility app to a premier experiential entertainment company. Bringing childhood dreams to life created a deep emotional impact. Furthermore, the experiences were so unique that guests posted millions of photos and videos online, generating an endless stream of free word-of-mouth marketing.

Visual appearance

The visuals showcase stunning, cinematic architectural photography of real-world structures. For example, the Up house is pictured suspended in the air by a massive crane against a bright blue sky, surrounded by thousands of colorful real balloons. The interiors are captured with incredible detail, matching the animated films perfectly.

Case Study 09

Heinz: Ketchup Insurance

What happened?

Heinz decided to tackle a universal, funny problem that almost every ketchup lover has experienced: accidentally splashing a bright red blotch of ketchup onto a clean shirt. They introduced a playful campaign offering "Ketchup Insurance," partnering with an on-demand cleaning service to cover the costs of removing stains for their customers. While it was a lighthearted joke, it operated like a genuine corporate insurance program.

Why it worked

The campaign succeeded because of its deep relatability. Heinz showed that they completely understand the everyday habits and struggles of their customers. By creating a clever, humorous solution to a minor downside of their own product, they turned a messy accident into a highly shareable, positive brand moment. Simple, lighthearted ideas based on real human experiences are often the ones that go viral most naturally.

Visual appearance

The advertisements look like serious, highly formal insurance documents and corporate brochures, subverted by funny imagery. They feature professional-looking models dressed in clean business attire, staring in comedic shock at vibrant red ketchup splatters across their crisp white shirts and luxury furniture.

Case Study 10

Barbie: Cultural Takeover Campaign

What happened?

To promote the Barbie movie, the marketing team went far beyond traditional trailers and posters. They executed a massive, synchronized cultural takeover by partnering with hundreds of different brands simultaneously. Suddenly, consumers could buy Barbie-themed clothing lines at major retailers, order pink burgers at restaurants, buy custom pink video game controllers, and stay in a real-life Malibu Dreamhouse.

Why it worked

This multi-channel approach made it almost impossible to ignore the movie, reaching people who might not have otherwise cared about the brand. They effectively claimed ownership of the color pink. Whenever anyone saw that specific shade of bright neon pink in public, their minds instantly connected it back to Barbie. A Truly great campaign doesn't just advertise within its industry; it blends directly into the surrounding culture through visual consistency and strategic partnerships.

Visual appearance

The entire campaign is dominated by a single, unmistakable shade of ultra-bright Malibu Barbie pink. Visuals feature bold, clean minimalist layouts, often displaying just the iconic cursive Barbie logo or a simple pink backdrop, letting the sheer power of the color palette do the branding work across billboards, retail displays, and social media.
